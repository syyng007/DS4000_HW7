Current Events & Challenges for Policymakers

Bolivia's policymakers face interconnected water challenges across the climate, economy, and governance spheres. The Amazon Cooperation Treaty Organization (ACTO), through its Amazon Basin Project (OCTA/UNEP/GEF), is installing glacier monitoring stations and developing water management models for La Paz and El Alto - proposed measures including network leak reduction, sustainable urban drainage, and household rainwater collection. However, Bolivia's economic ambitions create tensions: lithium extraction from the world's largest reserves demands approximately 2.2 million liters of water per ton, and plans to raise the hydroelectric share of the energy mix from 29% to 70% risk reshaping local hydrology and intensifying scarcity. The International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies (IFRC), one of the world's largest humanitarian networks, has warned thatdroughts and floods are causing long-term damage to food production, food security, and public health, with 11,000 dengue cases in the first quarter of 2024 alone. Transboundary cooperation is also urgent: the International Court of Justice's 2022 Silala River ruling urged Bolivia and Chile to cooperate over their shared water source and border, and the OTCA's Strategic Action Program - the region's first integrated water management framework - is increasingly critical as glacier retreat threatens to reduce Amazonian river flows by up to 20%.
