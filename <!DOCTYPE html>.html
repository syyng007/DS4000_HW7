Introduction

Today's topic is on water scarcity in the country of Bolivia. Bolivia is the most climate-vulnerable country in South America, and water scarcity is central to that vulnerability. Its Andean glaciers, which supply a critical share of freshwater to highland cities, farms, and hydropower systems, have lost 43% of their mass over the past 30 years, and the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) projects further declines will diminish water availability for agriculture, energy, and settlements across the region. The cities of La Paz and El Alto, home to 2.6 million people, depend heavily on glacial meltwater, with an estimated 1.5 million Bolivians relying on it during peak melting season. Bolivia's 2009 Constitution enshrines water access as a human right and bans its privatization, and Bolivia led the adoption of United Nations (UN) General Assembly Resolution 64/292 (2010), which recognized water and sanitation as a human right.

Bolivia's water governance was reshaped by the Cochabamba Water War of 2000, when citizens overturned the privatization of the city's water system through mass demostrations and protests. The upheaval led to the 2009 Constitution declaring water a human right and granting nature legal personhood, and the 2012 Law of the Rights of Mother Earth. Despite this framework, infrastructure remains weak: wastewater treatment rates are only about 30%, and glacial loss has been devastating. Since then the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) has installed advanced soil moisture sensors near El Alto at 4,500 meters to monitor glacial water dynamics, noting these could support early warning systems for drought and flooding.
